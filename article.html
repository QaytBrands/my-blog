The Pyramid Hiding in Plain Sight

Wealth is not a function of effort. It is a function of position.

This is the single most important sentence about economic life, and it is the one that polite society works hardest to obscure. The diligent shopkeeper, the sleepless founder, the doctor pulling twelve-hour shifts — all of them are participants in a topology they did not draw and cannot see. They have been taught that input produces output, that diligence produces wealth, that good people with good products win.

None of it is true at the level that matters.

What determines outcomes is where you sit in the flow of resources — not what you do once you are sitting there. Sit in the wrong place and you can work for forty years and remain replaceable. Sit in the right place and the system pays you a tax for existing.

This essay is about that topology. About the pyramid that organises every modern economy, the levels within it, and the specific class of insight required to climb between them. It applies to free-market capitalism inside a working democracy. It is addressed, in particular, to the people most punished by the current arrangement: the educated, hard-working, civically conscientious operators of small and medium businesses in India who have mistaken their own confusion for moral clarity.

I want to name that confusion plainly: it is structural ignorance dressed up as moral superiority.

The good citizen treats the government as an enemy, the politician as a goon, the stock market as a casino, and the businessman who courts power as a sellout. He thinks of each of these as a separate, vaguely contemptible thing. He is wrong. They are not separate things. They are the interconnected machinery by which resources are allocated in his country, and his refusal to engage with that machinery — his moral allergy to it — is precisely what keeps him at the bottom of the pyramid that the less squeamish are quietly climbing.

The Pyramid

The economy is not a flat marketplace of equal opportunity. It is a pyramid, and the rule of the pyramid is simple:

As you move up, competition decreases and leverage increases.

At each level, the question "what does this entity actually own?" produces a different answer. Owning the wrong thing locks you in place. Owning the right thing makes you a price-maker.

Levels 1 and 2 — Labour

Wage workers, hourly contractors, the gig economy. They sell time.

These levels are perfectly competitive. The skill is common, the worker is replaceable, and the price is set by whoever else is willing to do the same work for less. The defining feature of life here is mimetic competition — the René Girard insight that humans rarely know what they want, so they want what their neighbours want. This is why oversupply is permanent at the bottom: people enter the same trades, study for the same exams, apply for the same jobs, because the people around them did. The result is a structurally suppressed wage.

"Man is the creature who does not know what to desire, and he turns to others in order to make up his mind." — René Girard, Things Hidden Since the Foundation of the World

Level 3 — The Operator (SME, "Hard Worker")

The small or medium business owner. He owns assets — a shop, a workshop, machinery, inventory — but he does not own a gate or a pipe. The market sets his price. The customer can switch to one of a hundred identical operators tomorrow.

Three things define Level 3:

  1. The owner is the bottleneck. If he stops working, the business stops earning.
  2. He is a price-taker. He has no structural reason for any specific customer to choose him.
  3. He treats his business as a stack of chores rather than a system. "No one can do it as well as me" is the sentence that keeps him here.

He is the moral backbone of the economy and its primary victim. He subsidises his own enterprise with unpaid sweat equity, which the next level up converts into margin.

Level 4 — The Selector (Brand, Marketplace, Curator)

The Selector owns the relationship with the customer and the standard of what is good. He does not manufacture; he curates.

Amazon does not make products. Zomato does not cook food. Apple does not assemble phones. They do something more valuable: they decide which Level 3 operator gets to sell to the customer, on what terms, at what visibility, for what cut.

The mechanism is psychological anchoring. Once the customer trusts the brand or the platform, the brand becomes the mimetic mediator — the entity whose taste defines what is desirable. The Level 3 operator is reduced to a vendor competing for shelf space inside someone else's standard.

Is this just? Probably not. But the question is academic. The market does not consult anyone's ethics before assigning margin.

Level 5 — Sovereign Infrastructure (Pipes, Toll-booths)

These entities own the pipes through which the economy must flow: physical (ports, highways, power), digital (payment rails, cloud, telecom), or regulatory (licences, spectrum, exclusive concessions).

They are price-makers in the strongest sense. They do not sell products; they collect tolls — subscriptions, processing fees, transmission charges, statutory levies — different names for the same mechanism. They are, almost by construction, too big to fail, which means the state is structurally incentivised to keep them solvent.

Level 5 entities are not built. They are negotiated. They require capital, scale, and — decisively — alignment with the state.

Level 6 — The Politician

The politician is best understood here not as a moral agent but as a toll-booth business with a uniquely scarce structural asset: a vote-bearing seat that decides where public money flows. There is a finite number of these seats. The supply cannot be expanded by hard work, talent, or innovation. Whoever holds one is, by definition, sitting on a Level 5 asset.

This is not a defence of any particular politician. It is a description of what the position is.

Level 7 — The Political Party

A party is a collection of Level 6 assets under a unified brand and discipline. The structural advantages compound: agenda-setting, candidate selection, alliance leverage, narrative control, and access to the apparatus of the state itself. Level 7 is the highest aggregation of structural advantage available in a democratic society.

Levels 6 and 7 are not bolted onto the framework. They are its logical termination. The pyramid does not end where the private sector ends, because the rules that distribute resources do not end there either.

Why Level 3 Stays Stuck

Most readers of this essay are at Level 3 or aspiring to it. So the question that matters is: what keeps Level 3 at Level 3?

Three things, in roughly this order.

One — the chore mindset. Level 3 sees the business as a sequence of tasks to be done well. Level 5 sees it as a system to be designed once and operated by others. Until the operator stops being the smartest person in his own office, he cannot leave Level 3. He is Level 3.

Two — the fear of leverage. "Good citizens" distrust debt and distrust employees. They view loans as moral hazards and staff as expense lines. Level 5 understands that debt is the cheapest fuel for asset accumulation and talent is the only mechanism that decouples income from time. A Level 3 operator who refuses both will work hard until he dies, then die.

Three — the political allergy. The most expensive belief at Level 3 is that the government is the enemy. The government is in fact the largest co-investor in the country. It provides subsidies, concessional credit, infrastructure, demand, regulatory protection, and — through alignment with sovereign priorities — the regulatory shield that converts a private business into a strategic asset. Level 5 treats the state as a partner. Level 3 treats it as a predator and pays for that posture every quarter, in compliance friction, in lost tenders, in price competition that a permitted-and-protected competitor does not face.

If you think your customers are fools and your politicians are thieves, why are you running a business serving fools under regulation by thieves?

You aren't. You're running a business that has refused to understand the thing it is inside.

Structural Advantage: The Only Variable That Matters

Structural advantage is the bridge from Level 3 to Levels 4 and 5. It is the act of placing yourself at a bottleneck through which capital must flow. While Level 3 optimises for product quality (an internal property), Level 5 optimises for market topology (an external property).

The shift requires seven moves. They are not a checklist; they are a posture.

1. From chores to systems

Replace yourself with documented standard operating procedures. The business should be a legally binding machine that survives your absence, your boredom, your death. If you are indispensable, you are a Level 3 cost centre wearing the costume of an owner.

2. Radical leverage

Use debt to acquire assets. Use talent to acquire time. The Level 3 instinct is to minimise both. The Level 5 instinct is to maximise both, subject to discipline. The discipline is the point — leverage without discipline is a casino — but the absence of leverage is a guarantee of stagnation.

3. The state as co-investor

Find the intersection between your private profit and a sovereign priority — food security, employment, exports, industrial clusters, strategic autonomy — and sit on it. The most affordable capital in the country is allocated through that intersection. The competitor who refuses to find it is borrowing at retail rates against a permitted-and-protected rival.

4. Optimise for thinking margin

Hard work is a commodity; structural insight is a monopoly. Brutally cut the work that produces no leverage. The fourteen-hour day must shift from operating the mill to negotiating the toll gate. Silence and strategy are the highest-ROI uses of an owner's hour.

5. High-agency networking

Arguments with vendors, drivers, and customers are a status trap. Delegate conflict. Spend social capital upward — on the few high-value nodes (officials, financiers, large customers, peers two levels above you) whose decisions move outcomes by orders of magnitude. You are not looking for "more work." You are looking for permissions and alignments.

6. Sales as the definition of standard

Sales is not selling a product. Sales is the act of telling the market what to want. Whoever owns the sales channel becomes the mimetic mediator — the brand around which the market organises. From that position, the manufacturers (the Level 3s) work for you on terms you set.

7. Banking as a foundational relationship

Your relationship manager matters more than your plant manager. Credit is oxygen. Treat your bankers as partners in liquidity, not adversaries to be outwitted at renewal. A Level 5 business does not run on cash flow; it runs on access.

How to Tell If You Have It

Three diagnostic symptoms of a business that has crossed into structural advantage:

Inelasticity

You raise prices and the customer cannot leave. Either the switching cost is high, the alternatives are inferior, or the alternatives do not exist. This is the cleanest test.

High margin, low effort

The technology is mediocre, the team is small, the philosophy is unremarkable — and the business prints money anyway. Something is doing the work. Usually a network effect, a discovery bottleneck, or a regulatory moat.

Dependency accumulation

The customer depends on your trust. The competitor depends on your infrastructure. The state depends on your execution. When all three converge on a single entity, that entity has become the pipe, not the water flowing through it.

The Marwadi Case

The Indian business community most fluent in this framework is the Marwadi. Not because of mystical commercial DNA, but because they treat structural advantage as a collective project rather than an individual ambition. The four pillars are visible across generations and geographies:

  1. Own the bank. Co-operative and community-aligned banks finance their projects without dependence on adversarial credit committees.
  2. Own the law. Compliance ecosystems built to withstand challenge in the highest courts. Compliance is treated as armour, not as cost.
  3. Own the licence. Political funding as a structural input — not as charity, not as bribery, but as portfolio allocation toward the entities that issue permissions.
  4. Own the trust. Temples, schools, charitable trusts. Soft power produces hard advantages over decades.

This is not a Marwadi secret. It is available to any community that decides to organise itself around the pyramid instead of pretending the pyramid does not exist. The Marwadis are simply unembarrassed about it.

The Last Honest Sentence

There is a temptation, reading this, to feel that something has been said that should not be said. That the framework is cynical. That it justifies cronyism. That the moral preference for hard work, fair competition, and clean dealing is being mocked.

It is not. The framework does not say be a worse person. It says be an honest one.

The system you live inside is producing exactly the outcomes its incentives were designed to produce. Working harder inside it does not change the matrix; it only changes your output within a fixed matrix. The correct response is not more effort. The correct response is to identify whose interests the structure serves, and to position yourself in alignment with those interests rather than in protest against them.

You can change the game. You can play the game that is actually being played. What you cannot do is keep optimising for a game you wish existed and call the loss noble.

That is the choice. Everything else is commentary.
